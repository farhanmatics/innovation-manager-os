# Market Validation Framework

A structured approach to validating whether there is a real market for your product — before you build it.

## The Core Question

> Will someone pay money for this, and are there enough of them to build a business?

## Market Validation Funnel

```
Total Addressable Market (TAM)
    ↓
Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM)
    ↓
Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM)
    ↓
Early Adopter Segment
    ↓
Design Partners
    ↓
First Paying Customer
```

### TAM — Total Addressable Market
The total global revenue opportunity if you had 100% market share.

**How to estimate:**
- Number of potential customers globally × average contract value
- Use industry reports (Gartner, Forrester, IDC)
- Government procurement databases for public sector
- Trade association membership counts

### SAM — Serviceable Addressable Market
The portion of TAM you can realistically reach with your product and go-to-market model.

**Constraints:**
- Geographic reach (country, region, language)
- Industry vertical focus
- Company size (enterprise vs. SMB vs. government)
- Regulatory boundaries
- Partner/channel limitations

### SOM — Serviceable Obtainable Market
The portion of SAM you can realistically capture in 3–5 years.

**Factors:**
- Competitive intensity
- Sales capacity and cycle length
- Brand/reputation in target market
- Switching costs for customers
- Budget cycles (government = annual, enterprise = quarterly)

### Early Adopter Segment
The specific customer profile most likely to buy first.

**Characteristics:**
- Highest pain from the problem
- Already looking for a solution (active search)
- Budget allocated or easily accessible
- Decision-maker identifiable and reachable
- Referenceable (willing to be a case study)

## Buyer Analysis

### Identify the Economic Buyer
Who has the budget authority to purchase?

| Title | Typical Authority | Typical Budget |
|---|---|---|
| CIO / CTO | Technology purchases | $50K–$5M+ |
| COO | Operational tools | $25K–$1M |
| CISO / Security Director | Security solutions | $50K–$2M |
| Compliance Manager | Regulatory tools | $10K–$500K |
| VP of Customer Experience | CX platforms | $25K–$1M |
| Head of Operations | Workflow/automation | $10K–$500K |

### Identify the Buying Trigger

What specific event causes a customer to actively seek a solution?

**Common triggers:**

| Trigger Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Compliance Pressure** | New regulation, audit failure, certification requirement |
| **Cost Reduction Mandate** | Budget cut directive, operational efficiency target |
| **Security Incident** | Data breach, system compromise, near-miss |
| **Operational Failure** | System outage, SLA breach, customer churn spike |
| **Growth Constraint** | Can't scale with current tools, hiring bottleneck |
| **Competitive Pressure** | Competitor adopted technology, market expectations |
| **Leadership Change** | New executive with transformation mandate |
| **Contract Renewal** | Existing vendor contract ending, re-evaluation window |
| **Digital Transformation** | Board-mandated modernization initiative |

### Map the Buying Process

For enterprise sales, understand:

1. **Discovery**: How do they find solutions? (RFPs, analysts, peers, conferences)
2. **Evaluation**: What is their evaluation process? (POC, pilot, reference checks)
3. **Decision**: Who decides? (individual, committee, board)
4. **Procurement**: What is the procurement process? (direct, RFP, framework agreement)
5. **Implementation**: What is their implementation timeline?
6. **Budget**: What is their budget cycle? (annual, quarterly, project-based)

## Validation Methods

### Method 1: Problem Interviews (Pre-Product)

Interview 20–30 potential buyers. Do NOT pitch your solution. Only ask:

1. "Tell me about the last time you faced [problem]."
2. "What did you do about it?"
3. "What did that cost you (time, money, reputation)?"
4. "What would an ideal solution look like?"
5. "Have you looked for solutions? What did you find?"

**Success Signal:** 60%+ describe the problem with emotional language (frustration, fear, urgency).

### Method 2: Landing Page Test (Pre-Product)

1. Create a landing page describing the product
2. Drive targeted traffic (LinkedIn ads, industry newsletters)
3. Measure "Request Demo" or "Join Waitlist" conversion
4. Follow up with converters for interviews

**Success Signal:** 5%+ conversion rate from qualified traffic.

### Method 3: Letter of Intent (Pre-Build)

Ask 5–10 potential customers:

> "If we build this, would you sign a letter of intent to purchase at $X/year, contingent on the product meeting these specific requirements?"

**Success Signal:** 3+ signed LOIs at target price point.

### Method 4: Design Partner Program (During Build)

Recruit 3–5 customers who:
- Pay a reduced price during development
- Provide regular feedback
- Agree to be reference customers
- Influence the product roadmap

**Success Signal:** Design partners renew at full price after launch.

### Method 5: Pilot / Proof of Concept (Post-Build)

Deploy to 2–3 paying customers with:
- Defined success criteria
- Fixed evaluation period (30–90 days)
- Clear path to full deployment
- Executive sponsor on customer side

**Success Signal:** 2 of 3 convert to full deployment.

## Red Flags

- TAM < $100M (too small for VC-scale, may work for lifestyle business)
- No clear buying trigger (customers won't prioritize purchase)
- No budget owner identified (nobody can buy)
- LTV:CAC < 2:1 (unsustainable unit economics)
- < 3 potential design partners in network (can't validate)
- All feedback is polite but non-committal (no real pain)

## Green Flags

- Customers use emotional language about the problem
- Customers have tried to solve it themselves (hacked solutions)
- Regulatory or compliance pressure creates urgency
- Budget already allocated for "something like this"
- Multiple industries describe the same problem
- Reference customers in one industry are credible to another
